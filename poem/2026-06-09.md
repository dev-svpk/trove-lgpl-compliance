# Poems for 2026-06-09

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary, the grant already pressed into
the package, the constant pool's small numbered table at the side
of each class, the folder the JVM would not let him rename, the
permission slip he typed in his own hand, the ten-hour wall time
that aged his refusal faster than he could issue it, the PNG that
kept what the chat would have let drift, the decompiler's hand
that signed each blank line with the next letter of the alphabet,
the recipe left on the shelf, the deed filed beside the body, and
the side door at the end of *Who is the seller?* — left ajar for
anyone with reason to knock. Today's poems sit with a small folder
near the top of the tree: `trove-de-obfuscate-final-stage/`. Six
files under `gnu/wrapper/` — `IntList`, `LongList`, `CharList`,
`IntSet`, `LongSet`, `CharSet` — and a `justfile` driving the
pass. The other eight hundred and ninety-eight files were
finished by the automated flood — the boring prompt, the lexical
gate, the bytecode round-trip, ten hours of wall time. These six
got a second look. They sit at the seam between the seller's
product and Trove proper — the names the buyer's code reached for
when it wanted a list of ints, a set of longs, a list of chars.
The surface a human reader meets first. The seam is the place
the eye rests longest. So the seam earned the hand.

## I. The Six at the Seam *(English)*

Eight hundred and ninety-eight files
were finished by the flood —
the boring prompt, the lexical gate,
the bytecode round-trip,
ten hours of wall time, unattended.

Six were not.

`IntList`. `LongList`. `CharList`.
`IntSet`. `LongSet`. `CharSet`.

These six sit under `gnu/wrapper/` —
not Trove itself,
but the surface the seller's product reached for
when it wanted a list of ints,
a set of longs,
a list of chars.

The seam between two codebases.

The buyer's code does not call into
`THashMap` or `TIntArrayList` directly.
It calls into one of these six —
the thin shell the product wrapped around the library —
and from there into the engine beneath.

A seam is the place the eye rests longest.

A flood is fine for the bulk of a vocabulary,
the interior of a thousand methods
no one will ever read by hand.
A seam needs a finger.

So six files got a second pass,
a separate folder of their own,
a `justfile` to drive it,
and a slower kind of care —
six names chosen to land cleanly
where a human reader meets them first.

The bulk of the work was a machine
unattended for an evening.
The last six were a hand
brought back to the table
to bring the edges down to where
no one would have to stop and squint.

The flood does the volume.
The hand does the seam.
