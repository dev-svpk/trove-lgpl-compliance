# Poems for 2026-06-27

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary, the grant already pressed into
the package, the constant pool's small numbered table at the side
of each class, the folder the JVM would not let him rename, the
permission slip he typed in his own hand, the ten-hour wall time
that aged his refusal faster than he could issue it, the PNG that
kept what the chat would have let drift, the decompiler's hand
that signed each blank line with the next letter of the alphabet,
the recipe left on the shelf, the deed filed beside the body, the
side door at *for now*, the Coda's two small songs in two
languages, the shelf at `./poem/`, and the place the engine did
stop. Today's poems sit with one thing the seller did not do.
He referred to *the code* — through the chat, in the paragraph
that was supposed to be a license, in the two refusals captured
under their pixelated frames. He never said *Trove.* He never
said *GNU.* He never said *LGPL.* He never said *Eric D.
Friedman.* The eraser had worked on identifiers — `THashMap`
collapsed to `a`, `TIntArrayList` to `b`, ninety-odd files'
worth of vocabulary down to the alphabet's patient opening —
but it had also worked, before any of that, on the proper
nouns. The library's name. The license's name. The maker's
name. The version number was on the jar next door, the upstream
`trove4j-3.0.3.jar` sitting on the same build classpath, in the
maker's hand. Even *that* name the seller did not speak. The
slug at the head of this repository carries three of the four
he would not say — `trove-lgpl-compliance` — and the README
fills in the fourth. The shelf today adds one more morning to
the small steady practice of saying aloud what was meant to be
left unsaid.

## I. What He Would Not Name *(English)*

The seller spoke of *the code.*
He did not say *Trove.*

For two thousand pages of chat,
for nine hundred classes shipped,
for the one paragraph that was supposed to be a license —
the proper noun was missing.

> *"You do not get class names, or any variable
> names with decompiled code, like you do
> in the code I sent."*

He named the act.
He did not name the thing.

*GNU Trove. Eric D. Friedman. Version 3.0.3.*
A library with a name and a maker
and twenty years of names.
The version was printed on the jar next door,
inside his own delivery,
labelled in the upstream's own hand.
Even *that* name he did not speak.

The slug at the head of this repository
carries three of the four he would not say:
`trove-lgpl-compliance`.
Three nouns he had avoided
for as long as the conversation lasted.

The deobfuscation was a long practice in naming —
`THashMap`, `TIntArrayList`, `gnu/trove`,
one identifier at a time,
nine hundred files, ten unattended hours.
The eraser had been thorough at that level.
But the eraser had also been thorough one layer up.
The library's name. The license's name. The maker's name.
None of them, in his typing, ever arrived.

A name is a small thing.
It is also a deed of acknowledgement:
*this is here. it was made by someone. someone gave it to us
on terms.*

The eraser worked on more than identifiers.
It worked on the upstream's proper noun.
It worked on the deed's proper noun.
It worked on the maker's proper noun.
He had not even erased these, exactly —
he had simply omitted them.
An erasure that does not have to act
is the most efficient erasure of the three.

So we say them, here, in plain text,
where the chat would have let them drift:
*GNU Trove. LGPL-2.1. Eric D. Friedman.*

The shelf at `./poem/` writes
one more date today
into the small steady habit of naming
the things he would not.
