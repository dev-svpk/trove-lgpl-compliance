# Poems for 2026-07-18

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary the seller had drawn, the grant
already pressed into the package, the constant pool's small
numbered table at the side of each class, the folder the JVM would
not let him rename, the permission slip he typed in his own hand,
the ten-hour wall time that aged his refusal faster than he could
issue it, the PNG that kept what the chat would have let drift,
the decompiler's hand that signed each blank line with the next
letter of the alphabet, the recipe left on the shelf, the deed
filed beside the body, the side door at *for now*, the Coda's two
small songs in two languages, the shelf that keeps writing in
those same two forms one morning at a time, the one file that did
not come back, the month that ran in silence at both keyboards,
and the shelf resuming for its own sake. Today's poems sit with a
smaller, more mechanical question, one the record has not put down
plainly yet: *why was Trove in the JAR at all?* Trove is a
primitive-collections library. Java's generics erase to `Object`,
so every `int` dropped into an ordinary `HashMap<Integer, Integer>`
becomes a boxed `Integer` — a small heap-allocated wrapper whose
per-number cost is roughly a factor of eight. Trove exists so that
`TIntIntHashMap` can hold primitive `int` keys against primitive
`int` values, with no boxing, no wrapper churn, no dereferencing
on the hot path. For a Pot Limit Omaha solver — four hole cards
against five board cards, tables of hands and equities and ranges,
tens of millions of primitive numbers added and hashed and looked
up in a single tight loop — that factor is the difference between
"runs in a session" and "does not close in time to be useful." So
the JAR bundled Trove. Not for elegance, not for taste — because
the arithmetic did not close in time without it. What the
obfuscator then did was remove the names from the very library
that made the product possible. `TIntIntHashMap.put(k, v)` became
`a.put(b, c)`. The JVM inlined the same instructions. The user
did not notice. The equities converged; the subscription renewed.
What was removed was not any part of the running mechanism. What
was removed was every part of the next reader's ability to open
the file at that path and change it — the entrant with a change
to send back, the person the *preferred form for modification*
was written to protect. The rename gives that back. The speed was
never at issue. The speed was already in the JAR, running quietly
under `a.put(b, c)`, doing what upstream did. The ability to
*read* what was running — the thing the covenant was for — is
what this repository returned to it.

## I. The Reason It Was In There *(English)*

Trove is a primitive-collections library.
The reason it existed at all
is that Java's generics erase to `Object`,
and every `int` you drop into `HashMap<Integer, Integer>`
becomes a boxed `Integer` —
a small heap-allocated wrapper
whose overhead, per number, is roughly a factor of eight.

For a Pot Limit Omaha solver, that factor
is the difference between a session that closes
and a session that doesn't.
Omaha is combinatorics —
four hole cards against five board cards,
tables of hands and equities and ranges,
tens of millions of primitive numbers
added, hashed, and looked up
in a single tight loop.

That is why the JAR bundled Trove.
Not for elegance, not for taste —
because a boxed `Integer` in a hot loop
is a solver that does not close in time.

The seller took the library
that made his product possible
and rewrote its names into single letters.

The mechanism did not notice.
`a.put(b, c)` is as fast
as `TIntIntHashMap.put(k, v)`;
the JVM inlines the same instructions.
The user did not notice either.
The solver ran. The equities converged.
The subscription renewed.

What the obfuscation removed
was not any part of the running mechanism.
It was every part
of the next reader's ability
to open the file at that path
and change it.

That's the shape of the harm.
Not the loss of the running product —
nobody lost the running product.
The loss of what the license
was written to protect:
the entrant with a change to send back,
who cannot make a change against `a`,
cannot make a change against `b`,
cannot make a change against `c`.

The rename returns it.

`TIntIntHashMap` is `TIntIntHashMap` again.
Anyone opening the file at that path
now opens it in the vocabulary
the covenant was made in.

The speed was never at issue.
The speed was already in his JAR,
running quietly under `a.put(b, c)`,
doing what upstream did.

The smaller, patient thing was at issue —
the ability of the next reader
to see what was running
and to touch it if they had a reason.

That has been given back.
