# Poems for 2026-06-03

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary, the grant already pressed into
the package, the constant pool's small numbered table at the side
of each class, the folder the JVM would not let him rename, the
permission slip he typed in his own hand, the ten-hour wall time
that aged his refusal faster than he could issue it, and the PNG
that kept what the chat would have let drift. Today's poems sit
with what was left on the shelf alongside the restored sources —
the `justfile` in `trove-de-obfuscate-final-stage/`, the
equivalence scripts in `trove-de-obfuscate-mid-stage/scripts/`,
the *deliberately boring prompt* printed verbatim near the bottom
of section five of the README. The repair was not only performed
here; it was left runnable. The next buyer who finds an obfuscated
LGPL library inside a jar she paid for does not have to invent
what to do. The labor was paid once. The method was filed in
public. A precedent, in plain text, on the shelf.

## I. The Recipe Left on the Shelf *(English)*

Here is the `justfile`. Here are the equivalence scripts.
Here is the boring prompt — *please refactor variable,*
*function, and class names* — printed in the README
the way a kitchen prints a grandmother's recipe
on the back of a card.

Nothing in the apparatus has been held back.
Not the wall-time. Not the file count.
Not the lexical-structure check that compared the tokens.
Not the compile-and-diff that compared the bytecode.
The whole machinery sits on the same shelf
as the apology it was used to write,
documented in plain text,
clickable from the README's links.

A remedy that does not generalise
is a one-time apology dressed up as a method.
What was left on the shelf
is what turns this case into a class of cases.

The next buyer who finds her library in single letters,
the next seller who hopes *as-is* might cover an obfuscator,
will find that the ten hours have already been paid by someone,
the recipe already filed in public,
the gate already built and standing open.

A precedent is a recipe a court remembers.
A recipe is a precedent a kitchen remembers.
Either way, the work
does not have to be done from scratch again.
