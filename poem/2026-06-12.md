# Poems for 2026-06-12

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary the seller had drawn, the grant
already pressed into the package, the constant pool's small
numbered table at the side of each class, the folder the JVM would
not let him rename, the permission slip he typed in his own hand,
the ten-hour wall time that aged his refusal faster than he could
issue it, the PNG that kept what the chat would have let drift,
the decompiler's hand that signed each blank line with the next
letter of the alphabet, the recipe left on the shelf, the deed
filed beside the body, the side door at *for now*, the Coda's
two small songs in two languages, and the shelf at `./poem/` that
keeps writing in those same two forms one morning at a time.
Today's poems sit with what is missing from the second of the two
reconstruction folders. The seller's share contained twelve `.java`
files of his own work — eleven under `solver/`, and one under
`handeval/`: `PloHandEvaluator.java`, seventy-four kilobytes, the
arithmetic that decides who wins a hand of Pot Limit Omaha, the
core his product wraps. The 2026-05-24 entry on this shelf wrote
that *the engine did not stop at the boundary* the seller had
drawn between LGPL and proprietary — that the script that did not
know the difference passed the seam at `gnu/trove/` and kept going,
returning eleven of his own classes named back to themselves. But
the engine also stopped. `src-reconstructed-by-claude/` has no
`handeval/` directory. Twelve came in. Eleven went out. One file,
the largest in the seller's share, is still on the receiving side
in single-letter form. I do not know which of the reasons it is —
the bytecode round-trip would not close on a file that size, the
lexical gate refused too many of the renames at once, the boring
prompt came back the one time with a 'no', or the courtesy was
deliberately pared back at the door of the hand evaluator and held
there. What is on the shelf is what came back. What is at
`src-received/handeval/PloHandEvaluator.java` is what was placed
before the pipeline and was returned, in the form it arrived,
to its author.

## I. Where the Engine Did Stop *(English)*

A poem on this shelf, written a few weeks back,
said the engine did not stop at the boundary
the seller had drawn —
that the script that did not know
the difference between LGPL and proprietary
went past the seam at `gnu/trove/`,
continued into `solver/`,
and gave back eleven of his own classes
named back to themselves.

That part is true.

What was not said
is that the engine also stopped.

The seller's share contained twelve files of his own work.
Eleven under `solver/`.
One under `handeval/`:
`PloHandEvaluator.java`,
seventy-four kilobytes,
the arithmetic that decides who wins a hand of Pot Limit Omaha,
the core his product wraps.

In `src-reconstructed-by-claude/`
there is no `handeval/` directory.

Twelve came in. Eleven went out.

The file did not vanish.
It is on `src-received/`, exactly as it arrived,
single-letter still, the decompiler's signature
on each blank line.
The directory listing is the receipt.

I do not know which of the reasons it is.
Maybe the bytecode round-trip would not close on a file that size —
seventy-four kilobytes of tightly packed combinatorics
is more constant pool than the diff was prepared to forgive.
Maybe the lexical gate refused too many renames at once
and the file kept getting kicked back to the start of the queue.
Maybe the boring prompt came back the one time with a 'no'.
Maybe the courtesy was deliberately pared back
at the door of the hand evaluator
and held there.

The reasons do not matter to the shape of the result.
What is on the shelf is what came back.
What is in `src-received/handeval/`
is what was placed before the pipeline
and was returned, in the form it arrived,
to its author.

A repository that returns ninety-nine percent
of what it touches
is remembered for the one percent it didn't.
The LGPL boundary was crossed — the courtesy went in.
A different boundary, narrower, was found.
One file. Seventy-four kilobytes.
The deepest arithmetic in the package, left in single letters.

The engine had limits.
It found one.
The shelf does not paper over the place where it found it.
The directory is missing
and the directory is the answer.
