# Poems for 2026-06-02

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a fixed
rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine that
did not stop at the boundary, the grant already pressed into the
package, the constant pool's small numbered table at the side of
each class, the folder the JVM would not let him rename, the
permission slip the seller typed in his own hand, and the
screenshot that promoted his speech from chat to record. Today's
poems sit with the tool the seller called *source*. A decompiler
walks a compiled `.class` back to Java syntax — control flow,
types, method bodies, all reconstructed. At that level it does its
work well. But compilation throws the variable names away; the
bytecode keeps only the indices. When the decompiler walks back
through, every named slot is empty, and the decompiler is not
allowed to leave a slot empty. It writes in `a`. Then `b`. Then
`c`. The single letters in the files the seller delivered are not
Trove's variables. They are the decompiler's small unintended
novel — nine hundred chapters titled with single letters, authored
by software on the fly because there was nothing left to copy. The
seller delivered the novel and labelled it the source. What he was
passing off as the author's hand was the machine's.

## I. What the Decompiler Authored *(English)*

When the seller said *full source code*,
what he meant was the output of a tool.

A decompiler reads a compiled `.class`
and walks it back to Java syntax —
control flow, types, method bodies, all reconstructed.
At that level it does its work well.

But the compiled class no longer remembers
what the variables were called.
Compilation flattens names to indices;
the bytecode keeps the indices, drops the names.

So the decompiler, walking back,
finds an empty slot where each name used to be
and writes in `a`. Then `b`. Then `c`.
It is not allowed to leave the slot empty.

The single letters in those files
are not Trove's variables.
They are the decompiler's small unintended novel —
nine hundred chapters titled with single letters,
authored by software, on the fly,
because there was nothing left to copy.

The seller delivered the novel
and labelled it Trove.

What he was redistributing as *source*
was the decompiler's hand —
not Eric Friedman's,
not Rob Eden's,
not any human's choice of name.

The decompiler did its job correctly.
It restored every method body,
every branch, every cast.
It could not restore what had been thrown away.

What it did instead
was sign each blank line
with the next letter of the alphabet —
the way a notary fills a missing field
with the only mark available.

The seller mistook the signature
for the original work.

## II. 七言绝句 *(Chinese, Qiyan Jueju)*

| 中文 | English |
|---|---|
| 反编虽巧非原言， | The decompilation is skillful — yet not the original words; |
| 名失千千补甲篇。 | a thousand names lost; the chapters filled in with "jiǎ". |
| 售者捧之称所写， | The seller holds it up and calls it what was written; |
| 不知所写出机笺。 | unaware that what was written came from the machine's pen. |
