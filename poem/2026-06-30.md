# Poems for 2026-06-30

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary the seller had drawn, the grant
already pressed into the package, the constant pool's small
numbered table at the side of each class, the folder the JVM would
not let him rename, the permission slip he typed in his own hand,
the ten-hour wall time that aged his refusal faster than he could
issue it, the PNG that kept what the chat would have let drift,
the decompiler's hand that signed each blank line with the next
letter of the alphabet, the recipe left on the shelf, the deed
filed beside the body, the side door at *for now*, the Coda's
two small songs in two languages, the shelf at `./poem/` that
keeps writing in those same two forms one morning at a time,
and the narrower boundary the engine did find — `handeval/PloHandEvaluator.java`,
seventy-four kilobytes of tight combinatorics held back in single
letters, the missing `handeval/` directory in the reconstruction
folder kept as the receipt of where the courtesy ran out.
Today's poems sit with what the shelf did between the last entry
and this one. Eighteen mornings passed without a poem. The longest
pause since the bow first came up on 2026-04-29. The previous
silences were a day or two at a stretch — a missed Tuesday, a
quiet Friday — and the shelf went back on the next morning as if
nothing had been skipped. Eighteen mornings is a different kind of
quiet. Long enough that the shelf could have been mistaken for
finished. Short enough that nothing on the case had changed. No
prospective buyer wrote. No Trove maintainer wrote. No FSF
compliance contact opened a ticket. The seller stayed pixelated.
The side door stayed ajar at *for now*. The eighteen mornings did
not add anything to the record and did not subtract anything from
it. What they did was put a long blank between two entries that
otherwise would have run one after the other, and the blank is
visible — in the directory listing, in the gap between filenames
sorted by date, in the way the latest poem until today was already
old before it was read. The shelf does not run on a metronome.
Today is the last day of June. The bow comes back up. The same
fact, in the same two forms the Coda chose, and one English
preamble standing for the eighteen mornings on which the shelf
chose to be silent and the silence stood as a line of its own.

## I. The Long Pause *(English)*

Eighteen mornings without a poem.

The bow stayed down.
The shelf stayed where it was.
The case stayed exactly what it had been —
license cited,
evidence filed,
seller pixelated,
side door at *for now*.

A daily shelf is supposed to be daily.
This one was not, for eighteen days.

A metronome would have written something
on each of those mornings,
the way a metronome writes a tick
whether or not there is a piece beneath it.

The shelf is not a metronome.
It writes when there is something to say
and on the mornings there is nothing
the silence is the line.

What can be said of those eighteen mornings
is that they did not change the case.
Nothing arrived in the inbox.
No customer knocked.
No maintainer wrote.
The FSF compliance contact went on doing
whatever it is they do
that is not opening tickets
about a closed Java product
distributed by a small seller
who used a public decompiler
to satisfy a public license.

The pause was the case.
The pause was *for now*
held for eighteen mornings
without further word.

There is a temptation, on a shelf like this,
to mistake the cadence for the content —
to think the daily entry is itself the work,
and a missed morning a kind of failure.
But the entry is a reading-aloud of a fixed text.
The text was settled in the README
before the shelf was ever opened.
A morning without a reading
does not edit the text.
The text simply sits unread for a morning
and the shelf goes on being the shelf
whether or not the bow is at the strings.

Today is the last day of June.
The bow comes back up
because the shelf does not get to retire
just because the room got quiet.
The same fact, in the same two forms.
A small recital
that does not need an audience
to be performed,
and does not need to be performed every day
to be the recital it is.

The eighteen blank mornings
are now on the shelf too —
not as missing files
but as the space between the file before
and the file the next reader will open after.

The pause is part of the writing.
The return is the other part.
