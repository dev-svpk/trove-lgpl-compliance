# Poems for 2026-06-28

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary the seller had drawn, the grant
already pressed into the package, the constant pool's small
numbered table at the side of each class, the folder the JVM would
not let him rename, the permission slip he typed in his own hand,
the ten-hour wall time that aged his refusal faster than he could
issue it, the PNG that kept what the chat would have let drift,
the decompiler's hand that signed each blank line with the next
letter of the alphabet, the recipe left on the shelf, the deed
filed beside the body, the side door at *for now*, the Coda's two
small songs in two languages, the shelf at `./poem/` that keeps
writing in those same two forms one morning at a time, and the one
file the engine returned in single letters because that is the
form it arrived in. Today's poems sit with the letter at the
front of *LGPL*. The L stands for *Lesser*. The license is, by
design, the softer copyleft — the one the FSF wrote so a
closed-source product could link the library at all without
having to come out under the same terms. The full GPL would have
required the seller's own code — the solver, the hand evaluator,
the rest — to be opened under matching license. The Lesser was
the FSF's draft of *fine, do business, keep what is yours, link
the library however you like, sell the result.* The one piece of
work the Lesser kept on its own side of the ledger was the
library itself, in the form a human could modify, shipped beside
the binary that carried it. The seller had been handed a license
that already let him do almost everything he wanted. The single
narrow thing it asked for, he stripped on the way out. The
non-compliance was not at the line where the harder license drew
its boundary. It was one step inside of the line the lenient one
had already conceded.

## I. The L Is for Lesser *(English)*

The first letter of *LGPL* is *Lesser.*

It is the version of the GNU public license
the FSF wrote with a specific concession in mind:
that a library could be linked into a closed program
without forcing that program open.

The full GPL would have required the seller's product —
the solver, the hand evaluator, the rest of it —
to come out under the same terms.
That is what the Lesser was *lesser* of.

The trade was plain:
keep your product closed,
keep your source private,
keep your price what you like,
link the library however you wish,
sell the result.

The library asks for one thing in return —
its own source, in the form a human can modify,
shipped beside the binary that carries it.

The seller had been handed a license
that already let him do almost everything he wanted.
The L is the leniency.
It was the FSF's draft of
*fine, do business, keep what is yours.*

The one piece of work that was not his —
the names a human had written
into the Trove tree, twenty-some years before —
was the only thing left
on the LGPL's side of the ledger.

That was what he stripped on the way out.

It is worth saying plainly.
He was not refused.
He was permitted.
He had a license that pre-conceded
the commercial use of the work;
he had a permission slip
already softened past the harder forms of copyleft.

What remained was small, narrow, specific,
and one step inside of where the FSF had already drawn the line.

He could not honor what remained.

The L in LGPL did all the work it could on his behalf
before the license asked him for anything.
He still did not do the thing it asked.

What this shelf keeps recording, morning after morning,
is not that the license was strict.
It was the opposite.
The license was as lenient
as the FSF was willing to make it,
the year they put it out.

The non-compliance was not at the line of the harsher license.
It was a step inside of the line
the lenient one had already conceded.

A door that has already been propped open
can still be slammed
by the same hand
the propping was meant to free.
