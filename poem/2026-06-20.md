# Poems for 2026-06-20

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary, the grant already pressed into
the package, the constant pool's small numbered table at the side
of each class, the folder the JVM would not let him rename, the
permission slip he typed in his own hand, the ten-hour wall time
that aged his refusal faster than he could issue it, the PNG that
kept what the chat would have let drift, the decompiler's hand
that signed each blank line with the next letter of the alphabet,
the recipe left on the shelf, the deed filed beside the body, the
side door at *for now*, the Coda's two small songs in two
languages, the shelf at `./poem/` that keeps writing in those same
two forms one morning at a time, the missing `handeval/` directory
the engine did not return, the seam where the eye rests longest,
and the exception in the `.gitignore` that kept the case. Today's
poems sit with what stands above everything else on the README
page — above the title, above the prose, the first thing a reader
ever sees: `software-screenshot.png`. A poker solver
mid-calculation. A grid of cards across the screen. Ranges on
either side. Equities and frequencies in small type — the
percentages the engine returns when you ask it what to do. The
image is captioned in passing — *the product the seller shipped, a
poker solver that statically links GNU Trove* — but it carries the
weight the prose cannot. A JAR file on a hard drive is a fact a
debate can be had about: whether it was sent, paid for, run. A
screenshot is the program speaking out loud from inside the
buyer's machine. The solver is running. The columns are populated.
`gnu/trove/` is in there, statically linked, called every time the
engine asks the cards a question. If Trove were not running, the
picture would not exist. The picture exists. The README placed it
there for a reason — not as decoration, not as a marketing shot —
but as the evidentiary baseline the rest of the document rests on.
The argument begins with a fact you can see before you read.

## I. The Screenshot at the Head of the README *(English)*

Before the title,
before the prose,
before the table of paths,
before the link in the right-hand corner to the LICENSE,
there is a picture.

A poker solver, mid-calculation.
A grid of cards laid across the screen.
A range of holdings on either side.
Numbers in small type — equities, frequencies,
the percentages the engine returns
when you ask it what to do.

This is the seller's product.
This is what he ships.

The picture is the trigger.

The JAR on someone's hard drive
is technically the act of distribution,
but a JAR is a file,
and a file is something a debate can be had about —
whether it was sent,
whether it was paid for,
whether it ran.

A screenshot is not a file.
A screenshot is the program speaking out loud
from inside the buyer's machine.
The solver is running.
The columns are populated.
The decisions are being made,
one combo at a time.

`gnu/trove/` is in there,
statically linked,
called every time the engine
asks the cards a question.

If Trove were not running,
the picture would not exist.
The picture exists.
Therefore Trove was distributed.
Therefore the LGPL attached.
Therefore the source obligation came due.

The README put the picture
above the first paragraph
for a reason.
Not as decoration.
Not as a banner for the marketing page.

The picture is the evidentiary baseline
the rest of the document rests on.
The argument begins with a fact
you can see before you read.

Everything below the picture
is in service of what the picture has already shown:
the program was in someone's hands.
The library was inside the program.
The names had been stripped.
The covenant had been ignored.

The prose does not need to prove distribution.
The picture does that
before the prose begins to speak.

A repository can put its strongest exhibit
either at the top of the page
or at the end of the argument.
This one chose the top.
It chose the picture.
It chose the running solver,
mid-decision, cards on the screen —
because the loudest fact in the file
is that the program is alive,
and that aliveness is the trigger
no one in the chain can take back.
