# Poems for 2026-06-29

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary the seller had drawn, the grant
already pressed into the package, the constant pool's small numbered
table at the side of each class, the folder the JVM would not let
him rename, the permission slip he typed in his own hand, the
ten-hour wall time that aged his refusal faster than he could
issue it, the PNG that kept what the chat would have let drift,
the decompiler's hand that signed each blank line with the next
letter of the alphabet, the recipe left on the shelf, the deed
filed beside the body, the side door at *for now*, the Coda's
two small songs in two languages, the bow that keeps coming back
up, the six at the seam given a second hand, and the place where
the engine did stop — at `handeval/PloHandEvaluator.java`,
seventy-four kilobytes left in single letters. Today's poems sit
with the only image in the README that is not in the `evidence/`
folder: `software-screenshot.png`. It sits at the very top of the
document, above the prose, before the categories — captioned with
one line: *The product the seller shipped — a poker solver that
statically links GNU Trove.* Every other picture in the README is
pixelated — the seller's face under mosaic, his words under blur.
This one is unblurred. The thing he made is visible. He is not.
The shipment crossed the threshold the license names. The source
did not. The picture is the trigger. The picture is the proof.
The picture is also, for a moment, a frame of software working —
which was what all of this was meant to come down to in the end:
a thing that runs, and a thing that gives back to the work it
was built on.

## I. The Image at the Top *(English)*

The README opens with a picture.
Above the prose, above the citations,
above the file tree and the license note —
the only photograph in the document
that is not in the `evidence/` folder.

A screenshot of the product the seller shipped,
running on a screen,
glossy, clean,
the kind of frame a marketing page would use.

The caption is one line:
*The product the seller shipped —
a poker solver that statically links GNU Trove.*

Every other picture in the README is pixelated.
The seller's face under mosaic.
The seller's words under blur.
The polite refusal held behind a square of noise.

This one is not pixelated.
His work is visible. He is not.

The asymmetry is the case.

The screenshot proves what is otherwise hardest to see:
that distribution happened —
that the bytes left his hand and entered another machine,
which is the only event that makes the LGPL pull a trigger at all.
A license attaches to a delivery. The delivery is in the picture.

Without the image, the README would be an argument about a seller
in the abstract, a violation in principle.
With the image, the violation is dated, located, observable.
A real product. A real screen. A real moment of shipment.

The screenshot also sits outside the curation.
The `evidence/` folder is the part of the record
that knows it is evidence —
labeled, ordered, captioned for the chronology.
The screenshot at the top is not in any folder.
It is in the README itself.
It is what the reader sees first, before the case begins:
the thing in motion that the case is about.

There is a strange kindness in that placement.
The product is allowed to look like a product
before it is named as the trigger.
The seller's work gets one glossy frame on the page
before the rest of the page explains what it is missing.

What the screenshot does not show is `gnu/trove/`.
The single-letter names are not on the screen.
They are in the JAR
that is running underneath the pixels of the screenshot.

A reader who never opens a folder of the repo
still gets the whole shape of the case from the picture and the caption:
*statically links GNU Trove.*
That is the phrase that does the legal work.
*Static* — the library is part of the binary.
*Links* — the license follows the linkage.
*GNU Trove* — and Trove is LGPL.

The unblurred picture
beside the pixelated ones
is the line the document draws
between what gets protected and what does not.
The seller's identity is held under courtesy.
The seller's shipment is held up to the light.

Sooner or later, in the rhythm of the shelf,
the picture at the top of the README
had to take its turn at the page.
