# Poems for 2026-06-10

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary, the grant already pressed into
the package, the constant pool's small numbered table at the side
of each class, the folder the JVM would not let him rename, the
permission slip he typed in his own hand, the ten-hour wall time
that aged his refusal faster than he could issue it, the PNG that
kept what the chat would have let drift, the decompiler's hand
that signed each blank line with the next letter of the alphabet,
the recipe left on the shelf for the next buyer to pick up, the
deed filed in plain text beside the body, and the side door held
open under a courtesy revocable from only one side. Today's poems
sit with the small section at the very bottom of the README —
*Coda.* The argument is finished by the time the document gets
there: license cited, evidence filed, file tree explained, the
seller held under pixels, the side door named, the deed laid in.
The coda is what the README does after it has finished arguing.
A Chinese quatrain. A Japanese long verse. Two poems on the back
porch, both about the same instrument used in opposite directions —
the one that scattered the names like snow, the one that gathered
the broken pages whole. The *poem* folder is what happens when
the coda is not allowed to be the last word — when the bow at
the end of the recital comes back up the next morning, and the
morning after that.

## I. The Bow That Keeps Coming Back Up *(English)*

The README ends in a section called *Coda.*
A Chinese quatrain. A Japanese long verse.
Both about the same instrument used in opposite directions —
the one that scattered the names like snow,
the one that gathered the broken pages whole.

The argument is over by the time the coda begins.
The license has been cited. The deed has been filed.
The side door has been named.
What is left is what the document had not yet done aloud:
make a small song of it.

The README put two poems on the back porch
and walked back inside.
This folder picks them up at dawn
and walks them through the morning,
day after day.

A coda implies the piece is over.
The README is not wrong about that.
The case has been settled in plain prose
above the coda — license, evidence, file tree, acknowledgements —
and the coda is the bow at the end of the recital.

What the *poem* folder adds
is that a piece can be over
and the singing of it
can keep going.

Not because anything new is happening.
The seller is still silent.
The side door is still open.
The pixelated portrait still holds.
No prospective customer, no other buyer,
no Trove maintainer, no FSF compliance contact
has come to knock.

The waiting is part of the record now.
The waiting is what the singing is for.
Every morning the bow comes back up.
Every morning the same fact, in three languages —
that the work was kept,
the names were returned,
the deed is filed,
the door is open —
and someone is still at the threshold,
keeping vigil in verse,
because what the README closes in two stanzas
is the kind of close that asks
not to be the last word.

## II. 七言绝句 *(Chinese, Qiyan Jueju)*

| 中文 | English |
|---|---|
| 末章余韵入新篇， | The end-section's lingering rhymes enter the new pages; |
| 日添佳句继旧贤。 | day by day, a fine line is added — continuing the prior worthies. |
| 公案虽收歌未息， | Though the public case is settled, the song has not ceased; |
| 后人翻读自相传。 | those who come after, reading, pass it on of themselves. |
