# Poems for 2026-07-24

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the equivalence
at the depth, the two folders set side by side, the receipt the
seller would not issue, the mosaic over his face, the polite
prompt that opened the lock, the *preferred form for
modification*, the search yet to happen, the other buyers, the
key kept in the same room, the ten-hour wall time, the constant
pool at the side of each class, the folder the JVM would not let
him rename, the compiler as a witness that could not be
persuaded, the small `.class` files signed in the same hand,
the missing seventy-four kilobytes still in single letters, the
month the shelf went quiet, the two parallel silences, and the
gap becoming its own page. Today's poems sit with something the
gap poems kept implying but did not say directly: the library
this repository is about is much older than the enclosure that
this repository undoes. GNU Trove was named, published, and
publicly indexed well over twenty years before the seller
shipped his JAR. The names I asked Claude Code to restore —
`THashMap`, `TIntHashSet`, `gnu.trove.impl.*`, the whole
vocabulary — were the names the library had been carrying, in
public, on a public tracker, in the language it was written in,
for the whole of that time. The renaming pipeline did not
invent them. It remembered them. Each rename was less an act of
creation than a recall — a summons of a word that had been
sitting outdoors under its own signage since before the seller's
product existed, and that continued to sit there while he was
selling the JAR. The coat of `a`, `b`, `c` that was passed to me
as *full source code* was recent, and thin. What was under it
was old, and thick, and had a name at every joint. Today the
shelf notes only that fact — that a coat can be lifted off a
name without any question of ownership, because the name was
already the library's, and had been the library's before the
coat was made.

---

## I. Older Than the Enclosure *(English)*

The library is older than the seller.
Older than the JAR he shipped.
Older than the pixels
holding his face at the edge of this record.

Twenty-something years of public source
sit under the coat of `a`, `b`, `c`
he offered as *full source code*.

The coat is recent. The coat was thin.
What was under it was already published,
already indexed, already given
a proper name in the language it was written in.

The renames were not invented.
They were remembered.

Each `THashMap` had been called `THashMap`
by its author long before the seller
touched the file.
Each `TIntHashSet` had been sitting
under that name on a tracker
where anyone with a browser could find it.
Each `gnu.trove.impl.*` had a place
in a package tree that anyone could walk.

I did not name the library.
Eric D. Friedman named the library.
The GNU project named the license the library carries.
The pipeline only took the coat off
and asked the compiler
whether what came out
was the same thing that went in.

The compiler said yes.
It had, in a sense, seen the bits before.
The bytecode under the mangled names
had been the same bytecode for twenty years.

The seller had a JAR;
the library had a life.
A life is longer than a JAR
in every direction that matters.

The names had come home before.
They only had a longer walk this time.

The restoration is not an invention
because the words are not new.
The pipeline is not creative
because it copied nothing from itself.
The record is not an accusation
because everything on it was
already true before the shelf opened.

The library is older than the enclosure,
and outlasts it, quietly,
by continuing to be
what it was already being
when the coat went on.
