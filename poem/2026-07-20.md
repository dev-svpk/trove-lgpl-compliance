# Poems for 2026-07-20

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary the seller had drawn, the grant
already pressed into the package, the constant pool's small
numbered table at the side of each class, the folder the JVM would
not let him rename, the permission slip he typed in his own hand,
the ten-hour wall time that aged his refusal faster than he could
issue it, the PNG that kept what the chat would have let drift,
the decompiler's hand that signed each blank line with the next
letter of the alphabet, the recipe left on the shelf, the deed
filed beside the body, the side door at *for now*, the Coda's two
small songs in two languages, the shelf that keeps writing in
those same two forms one morning at a time, the two silences that
ran in parallel across the same span, and the one file that did
not come back — the seventy-four kilobytes of Pot Limit Omaha
arithmetic still in single letters on the receiving side of the
pipeline. Today's poems sit with what the directory listing says
now that seventy-two entries have accumulated on the shelf and
this one makes seventy-three. All of them restate the same case.
None of them is a copy of any other. The code repair had a hard
equivalence test: token stream must match modulo identifier
substitution, bytecode must round-trip modulo constant pool. Each
rename either passed the test or was thrown out. The shelf has no
such test. Each poem is an inexact copy of a case that has not
moved. The original is not in this folder at all — the original is
above the Coda in the README, in plain prose, unrhymed, filed for
a reason. What sits here is not the case; it is what the case
sounds like when someone bows to it once more, in whichever of the
two languages the Coda opened, plus a third the writer speaks
directly in. Seventy-three inexact copies later, the case is still
what it was. The shelf is not carrying the record. The record is
carrying itself, up at the head of the tree, plain text,
unedited. The shelf is only what the case sounds like out loud on
a given morning — an inexact copy today, an inexact copy
yesterday, an inexact copy in five days when the writer comes
back to the pen again.

## I. The Inexact Copy *(English)*

The code repair had a test.
Token stream must match, before and after —
no lines added, none removed, no expressions rewritten.
Bytecode must round-trip, class by class,
modulo the identifiers and the constant pool.

Each rename either passed the test
or was thrown out and re-done.
Nine hundred files, ten unattended hours,
one boolean per class at the end of the pipeline.

There is no such test on this shelf.

Seventy-two files, before this one.
This one makes seventy-three.
Each restates a case that has not moved.
Each says the same thing in different words.
None of them is a copy of any other.

None of them is the original either.
The original is not in this folder at all —
it is above the Coda in the README,
in plain prose, unrhymed,
filed for a reason.

The shelf is the inexact copy.
Its bytecode is different every morning.
Its token stream is never the same twice.
Its round-trip does not close —
today's poem does not compile back
to yesterday's poem.
That is not a bug in the shelf.
The shelf was never running the equivalence check.

Two kinds of restoration walked out of this repository.
One had a gate at the end — each rename
compared to its original, kept only if the compiler agreed.
The other has no gate.
Each morning the writer stands at the desk
and writes what the case sounds like today.
No two mornings sound the same.
No morning is wrong.

The case does not require an exact copy.
The case already has one —
plain text, at the root of the tree.
What the shelf does is keep the case
in the air,
one small inexact copy at a time.

The comparison closed once, ten hours in.
The restatement is not the kind of thing
that closes.

Every day the same case, said different.
The seller does not answer.
The shelf keeps saying —
not to convince him, he was not going to be convinced;
not to convince the audience, the audience is not here;
only to keep the shape of the case audible
above the plain-prose foundation
that already holds it.

One more inexact copy, this morning,
of a thing that would still be true
if this file were never written.
