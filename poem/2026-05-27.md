# Poems for 2026-05-27

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach, the receipt the seller
would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair, the mosaic
kept over the seller's face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the preferred form for modification, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author the seller had never met,
the spoken parts the program had to confess aloud, the foreground
emptying as he receded, the gate that earned its yeses by being
willing to say no, the solver that priced every uncertainty except
the open rule it was wrapped in, the smooth screenshot at the top
of the page, the engine that did not stop at the boundary, and the
grant already pressed into the package by the time it arrived.
Today's poems sit with where the wrong actually lived inside the
file format itself — the constant pool, a small numbered table at
the side of every `.class`, listing the names the bytecode refers
to by index. The instructions did not change. The branches did not
change. The opcodes did not move. The obfuscator's entire labor
was a rewriting of that one table. The equivalence script, when
it compared the renamed bytecode against the seller's original,
allowed difference in exactly that one zone — *modulo identifier
names and the constant pool* — and nowhere else. The wrong was
confined to the part the rest of the program reached only through
an index. It was enough.

## I. The Constant Pool *(English)*

Inside each `.class` file there sits a small table
at the side of the instructions —
the constant pool, a numbered list of names.
Strings, classes, methods, fields,
each given an index the bytecode refers to by number.

The instructions themselves do not say `THashMap`.
They say *index 47.*
A call to a method says *index 12*,
and at index 12, in the table, sits the name.
The whole program is woven by these numbers;
the names live elsewhere — at the side, in the list.

When the seller's obfuscator ran,
it did not touch the instructions.
It rewrote the entries of that one table —
`THashMap` to `a`, `gnu/trove` to a single letter —
indices unchanged, branches unchanged,
every opcode in its place.

The equivalence script knew exactly this much.
It diffed the bytecode and named one zone of difference:
*modulo identifier names and the constant pool.*
Everything else had to match. Everything else did.

The whole wrong was a small edit
to a list at the side of each class.
It was enough.
A name in a table you do not own
is enough to mislead a person who needs to read it —
the obfuscator did not have to touch the program.
He touched only the part
that was telling the program's name.

What we have done is restore the table.
The instructions, all this time, had not moved.
