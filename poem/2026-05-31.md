# Poems for 2026-05-31

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary, the grant already pressed into
the package, the constant pool's small numbered table at the side
of each class, the folder the JVM would not let him rename, and
the sentence the seller typed in a chat that turned out to be the
permission slip for the very act of redistribution this repository
performs. Today's poems sit with the half-line at the bottom of
section five of the README: *~10 hours of wall time.* The whole
repair — the ~900 files, the renames, the lexical-structure check,
the bytecode round-trip — end-to-end, fully automated. Ten hours.
The cost the seller's refusal was presumably priced against had
already collapsed by the time he refused. He was remembering an
older world, where putting a single obfuscated library back
together was patient human labor — months of careful work, a
piece of a career, a budget line. He was right about that older
world. He was several years late in noticing that it was gone.
His refusal aged faster than he could issue it. The license could
be honored between dinner and the late news.

## I. Ten Hours *(English)*

The argument took longer than the work.

The back-and-forth with him —
the *no* by chat,
the second screenshot,
the doubled-down second *no* —
took an afternoon all its own.

The repair took ten hours of wall time.
Nine hundred files, unattended.
A script that did not have to be persuaded,
did not have to be asked twice,
did not have its pride in the way.

He had refused to spend the hours.
He must have thought they would cost him more
than he was willing to give.
He must have been remembering the older world,
where deobfuscation was patient human labor —
months of work to recover a vocabulary,
budgets, careers, slow restoration of a name.

That world was already gone the day he refused.
The cost he was protecting against
had collapsed to ten hours of machine time
on a desk in a small apartment.

His refusal aged faster
than he could issue it.

The thing he thought was expensive to undo
was no longer expensive to undo.
The license could be honored
between dinner and the late news.

The ten-hour figure is not a boast.
It is the measure of how badly he misread
the cost he was hiding behind.
