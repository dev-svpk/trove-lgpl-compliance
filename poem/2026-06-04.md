# Poems for 2026-06-04

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary, the grant already pressed into
the package, the constant pool's small numbered table at the side
of each class, the folder the JVM would not let him rename, the
permission slip the seller typed in his own hand, the ten-hour
wall time that aged his refusal faster than he could issue it,
the PNG that kept what the chat would have let drift, and the
recipe left runnable on the shelf for the next buyer to come.
Today's poems sit with the small text file at the top of this
repository's tree. `LICENSE`, in capitals — the GNU Lesser
General Public License, version 2.1, verbatim, the same document
the FSF published in 1999. The LGPL asks for two things, not one:
the preferred form for modification, and a copy of the covenant
itself, shipped beside the binary so the recipient can read the
rules without leaving the package. The seller delivered neither.
The grant attached anyway, by the simple fact of distribution;
the file is something else — the recipient's copy of the rules,
in language they can read, sitting next to the work the rules
govern. The first requirement was the form. The second is the
text. Both were missing from the share. Both are present here.

## I. The Deed Filed Beside the Body *(English)*

The LGPL asks for two things, not one.

The first is the preferred form for modification —
source as a human keeps it, names intact.
That has been talked about already on this shelf.

The second is the text of the license itself —
a copy of the covenant, in plain prose,
shipped beside the binary
so the recipient can read the rules
without leaving the package.

The seller did not include it.
No `LICENSE` file in the share.
No copy of the deed.
No naming of the clauses.
No pointer to the document
the buyer was already governed by.

The grant attached anyway —
that part was settled on the 26th of May,
the covenant in force at the recipient's address
whether the seller said so or not.

But a covenant in force
and a covenant a person can read
are not the same convenience.
The first does the legal work.
The second tells the next reader what is permitted —
copy, modify, redistribute, on these terms —
in language the bytecode does not provide.

In this repository the file is at the top of the tree.
`LICENSE`, in capitals,
the GNU Lesser General Public License, version 2.1,
verbatim,
the same document the FSF published in 1999.

It is not new work.
It is one of the smallest contributions on this shelf —
a file the seller could have copied with a single command,
and did not.

The omission was about the size of a file.
The fix is about the size of a file.
The deed sits beside the body now,
in plain text, at the top of the tree,
where a recipient who wants to know the rules
can find them named,
numbered,
and in force.

## II. 七言绝句 *(Chinese, Qiyan Jueju)*

| 中文 | English |
|---|---|
| 售包当日缺一章， | His parcel, on that day, was missing one chapter; |
| 许可之文不在旁。 | the text of the license was not at its side. |
| 今编补置归书首， | Now, supplied, it is placed at the head of the book; |
