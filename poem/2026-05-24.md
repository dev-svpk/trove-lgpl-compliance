# Poems for 2026-05-24

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach, the receipt the seller
would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair, the mosaic
kept over the seller's face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the preferred form for modification, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author the seller had never met,
the spoken parts the program had to confess aloud, the foreground
emptying as he receded, the gate that earned its yeses by being
willing to say no, the solver that priced every uncertainty except
the open rule it was wrapped in, and the smooth screenshot at the
top of the page. Today's poems sit with the second folder
alongside `gnu/trove/` — `src-reconstructed-by-claude/`. The
pipeline had no opinion about scope. It accepted the seller's own
product files — `solver/`, his proprietary work, the part he had
not been obliged to share — and put them through the same renames,
the same equivalence checks, the same patient diff. What the LGPL
required was the Trove portion. What the engine returned, having
no boundary written into it, was both.

## I. The Engine That Did Not Stop *(English)*

The license required only the `gnu/trove/` part.
The pipeline had no opinion about scope.
It accepted any folder pointed at it
and gave back the same patient diff —
the bytecode round-trip, modulo names.

So the seller's own files went through it too.
Eleven classes in `solver/`, his proprietary work,
the part he had not been obliged to share —
the part he had stripped down to the alphabet
because he wanted to keep it.

The script that did not know
the difference between LGPL and proprietary
did not stop at the boundary
the seller had drawn between them.

A boundary held in place
by the cost of crossing it
does not survive the cost coming down.

What he meant to protect by stripping
he protected only against an older world.
The same engine that gave back what he owed
gave back, beside it, what he had wanted to keep.

This was not vengeance.
There is no vengeance in a script
that compiles, diffs, and moves on.
It is only what the work did
when you set it on the table
and let it run to the end of the folder.
