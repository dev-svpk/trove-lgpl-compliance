# Poems for 2026-07-16

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary the seller had drawn, the grant
already pressed into the package, the constant pool's small
numbered table at the side of each class, the folder the JVM would
not let him rename, the permission slip he typed in his own hand,
the ten-hour wall time that aged his refusal faster than he could
issue it, the PNG that kept what the chat would have let drift,
the decompiler's hand that signed each blank line with the next
letter of the alphabet, the recipe left on the shelf, the deed
filed beside the body, the side door at *for now*, the Coda's two
small songs in two languages, the shelf at `./poem/` that keeps
writing in those same two forms one morning at a time, the vigil
that outlasted its own interruption, and the two silences that ran
in parallel across a month of unbroken public air. Today's poems
move off the pause. They sit with what the pipeline was never in a
position to give back. The rename brought nine hundred class names
home. It did not bring the sentences above them. Decompilers open
the door of the class and leave every prose explanation outside on
the step: the class Javadoc, the field notes, the small warnings
above each hash strategy, the copyright header naming Eric D.
Friedman and the year, the reasoning for the load factor, the
caveat about the primitive iterator that a maintainer would have
wanted the reader to see. Bytecode does not carry any of it — the
compiler consumes those characters and emits nothing that records
they were there. When the token-stream check compared before and
after, both sides were quiet at the top of the class, and the
check did not know one of them used to speak. The restoration
closed on the shape because the shape was what the intermediate
form was willing to carry. What the shape does not include is not
missing from the restoration — it was missing from what the
seller shipped to begin with. The `LGPL-2.1` calls for the
*preferred form for modification*. The renamed sources give a
maintainer back the skeleton on which to modify. For the author's
voice above each class, the reader still has to open upstream. The
distance from here to upstream is one URL long, and that URL was
also the one the pipeline held open while it read.

## I. The Voice the Rename Could Not Reach *(English)*

The rename brought back nine hundred class names.
It could not bring back the sentences above them.

Bytecode does not keep what the author said in prose.
The compiler consumed those characters and emitted
nothing that recorded they were there.
Decompilers open the door of the class
and leave every explanation outside on the step —
the class Javadoc, the field notes,
the small warnings above each hash strategy,
the copyright line under which Eric D. Friedman
had written, in the year the library was released,
what the class was for.

The token-stream check
compared silence to silence at the top of the class,
and passed.
Neither side was speaking.
The check did not know
one of them used to speak.

What restoration reached:
the shape of the map, the arity of the method,
the type of the field, the position of every argument,
the class hierarchy, the exception a call might throw,
the constant pool with its small numbered table on the side.

What restoration could not reach:
the author's line above `THashMap`,
the reasoning behind the load factor,
the caveat about the primitive iterator,
the note left over the hash strategy
so the next maintainer would not undo
the small correctness the previous one had built in.

The restoration closed on the shape
because the shape was what the intermediate form was willing to carry.
What the shape does not include
is not missing from the restoration —
it was missing from what the seller shipped to begin with.
The decompiler dropped the prose
before the rename pipeline was ever asked to look at the file.
The shelf inherits the silence at the top of each class
from the tool the seller ran on his own end.

A restoration that closes on the shape is still a restoration.
The JVM cares about the shape.
The compiler cares about the shape.
The next maintainer, opening a renamed file to make a change,
gets back the skeleton on which to make it.
The *preferred form for modification*
is bounded, in practice, by what survived transit —
and prose does not survive transit
through the compiler and back out the decompiler.

Upstream still has the sentences.
Upstream still has the copyright header,
the class Javadoc, the small warnings,
the maker's voice above each hash strategy,
undisturbed by the seller's pipeline
because the seller never touched that copy.

The shelf will not pretend the restoration was total.
It gave the class its name.
It did not give the class its author's voice.
For the voice, the reader still opens upstream.
The distance from here to upstream is one URL long,
and that URL was also the one
the pipeline held open while it read.

## II. 七言绝句 *(Chinese, Qiyan Jueju)*

| 中文 | English |
|---|---|
| 反编空得骨中痕， | The decompiler retains only traces within the bones; |
| 名复形还意未陈。 | names restored, form returned — yet the intent is not laid out. |
| 先生序注从谁问， | The master's prefaces and notes — from whom shall I ask? |
| 唯向原源见故人。 | Only turning to the original source do I meet the old friend. |
