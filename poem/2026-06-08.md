# Poems for 2026-06-08

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary, the grant already pressed into
the package, the constant pool's small numbered table at the side
of each class, the folder the JVM would not let him rename, the
permission slip he typed in his own hand, the ten-hour wall time
that aged his refusal faster than he could issue it, the PNG that
kept what the chat would have let drift, the decompiler's hand
that signed each blank line with the next letter of the alphabet,
the recipe left on the shelf — the `justfile`, the equivalence
scripts, the boring prompt printed in plain text — so the next
buyer would not have to rebuild the apparatus from scratch, the
deed filed beside the body — the LGPL text itself, supplied
verbatim at the top of the tree where the seller's package had
no copy of the covenant it was governed by, and the side door
held open under three small words at the end of *Who is the
seller?* — *for now* — the seller's name pixelated and held but
not lost, the courtesy revocable from only one side. Today's
poems sit with the smallest folder in this repository.
`trove-de-obfuscate-final-stage/` — six classes deep, plus a
`justfile`. The bulk pass, in `trove-de-obfuscate-mid-stage/`,
handled 904 files in lexical lockstep, each rename gated by the
token-stream check and the bytecode round-trip. It did not finish
in a single sweep. Six files remained — `gnu/wrapper/` —
`IntList`, `LongList`, `CharList`, `IntSet`, `LongSet`, `CharSet`
— and earned a folder of their own. The wrappers are the polite
face Trove turns to the rest of Java: thin shells that present
the primitive collections behind a `java.util.List` or
`java.util.Set` interface so a caller can reach the library
without leaving the familiar. Their bodies referred to the
canonical names the mid-stage pass was still establishing.
The floor had to be named before the surface. The pipeline
admitted this in the only way a directory tree can — by
splitting itself into two stages and naming them.

## I. The Final Six *(English)*

The mid-stage pass handled nine hundred and four files.
`THashMap`, `TIntArrayList`, `TLongObjectIterator`,
the deep machinery of the library —
renamed in lexical lockstep,
token streams matched before and after,
each class recompiled and bytecode-diffed
against the seller's original.

Then it stopped.

Six files remained, in `gnu/wrapper/`:
`IntList`. `LongList`. `CharList`.
`IntSet`. `LongSet`. `CharSet`.

Not the engine. Not the data structures.
The thin shells over them —
the adapters Trove keeps at its surface
so a caller using `java.util.List`
can reach the primitive collections
without leaving the familiar.

These six earned a folder of their own:
`trove-de-obfuscate-final-stage/`,
a `justfile` to drive the pass,
the smallest deliberate finish in the whole tree.

The bulk pass could not close them in one sweep.
Their bodies referred to the canonical names
the earlier pass was still establishing —
a wrapper holds its referent inside its method bodies,
and you cannot rename what your referent is called
until your referent has been called something stable.

Name the floor first.
Then name the surface.

There is a kind of work that scales
and a kind of work that does not,
and the boundary between them is exactly
where the wrappers begin —
where Trove leaves its own dialect
and starts speaking standard Java.

The seam between *what was written* and *what was used*
carries six labels.
A caller program that imports Trove at all
usually imports it through these.

Until those six names returned,
the deobfuscation was not finished.
The mid-stage was the labor.
The final stage was the closing latch.
Six small handles, named back to themselves,
and the library, end to end, could be read.
