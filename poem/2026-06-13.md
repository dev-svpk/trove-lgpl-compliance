# Poems for 2026-06-13

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary the seller had drawn, the grant
already pressed into the package, the constant pool's small
numbered table at the side of each class, the folder the JVM would
not let him rename, the permission slip he typed in his own hand,
the ten-hour wall time that aged his refusal faster than he could
issue it, the PNG that kept what the chat would have let drift,
the decompiler's hand that signed each blank line with the next
letter of the alphabet, the recipe left on the shelf, the deed
filed beside the body, the side door at *for now*, the Coda's
two small songs in two languages, the shelf at `./poem/` that
keeps writing in those same two forms one morning at a time, and
the file the engine declined to forward — `handeval/PloHandEvaluator.java`,
seventy-four kilobytes of pot-limit-omaha arithmetic returned to
its author in single letters. Today's poems sit with two small
hyphenated words near the front of the seller's own sentence
about what he had sold. *"The code is sold as-is, and is free
to be redistributed, resold or used however they see fit."*
The phrase *as-is* is a warranty disclaimer — a small standard
panel software sellers hang on the wall to keep future quality
claims from finding a doorway. It is not a license. It does not
grant rights; it waives them. It does not displace the LGPL
already sitting under `gnu/trove/` inside the JAR he was selling
— a cover sheet a previous author had stapled to the package
twenty years ago, in a piece of legalese sturdier than two
hyphenated words. The seller spoke a familiar language — the
standard reassurance of the marketplace — but the wrong dialect
for the building he was inside. A license grants. A disclaimer
waives. He used the second where only the first would have done
the work.

## I. "As-Is" Was Not a License *(English)*

The seller put it in writing:
*"The code is sold as-is, and is free to be redistributed,
resold or used however they see fit."*

Two words, near the front of that sentence,
were doing more work than two words can carry.

*As-is* is a warranty disclaimer.
A boilerplate. A small standard panel
software sellers hang on the wall
to keep future claims about quality
from finding a doorway.

The seller hung the panel
and walked away from the rest of the building.

*As-is* does not grant.
*As-is* does not license.
*As-is* does not displace the LGPL
already pre-applied to nine hundred files
under `gnu/trove/` inside the JAR he was selling —
a cover sheet a previous author had stapled to the package
twenty years ago,
in a piece of legalese sturdier
than two hyphenated words.

The seller spoke a familiar language —
the standard reassurance of the marketplace —
but the wrong dialect
for the building he was inside.

A license grants rights.
A warranty disclaimer waives them.
The seller used the second
and meant something only the first could mean.

The two words sat at the front of his contract
like a small wooden sign
nailed crookedly across a door —
and the door was not the kind of door
a sign across it could close.

Inside, the LGPL kept its standing,
written into the package, not over its lintel.
*As-is* hung where the seller had placed it,
covering nothing.

If the building had been the seller's all the way through,
*as-is* would have been doing its proper job —
saying *I make no promise that anything inside
works, or fits your purpose, or is fit at all* —
and that would have been fine.
A buyer would accept the disclaimer
or look for a different building.

But the building was a federation:
the seller's own rooms above,
GNU Trove's nine hundred rooms below,
with a license clearly posted at the stairwell
indicating the second flight had its own terms.

A disclaimer at the front entrance
does not republish the notice at the stairwell.

The seller's two words
described what he would not warrant.
They did not describe
what he was permitted to ship.
