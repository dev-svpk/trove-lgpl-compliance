# Poems for 2026-05-28

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, and the smooth surface of the running product. Today's
poems sit with the one credit the obfuscator could not strip — the
package path itself. He scraped the class names down to single
letters, but `gnu/trove/` stayed where it was, because the JVM's
class loader will not run a class whose package it cannot find.
The folder name was load-bearing. `gnu` for Project GNU, `trove`
for the library — a two-word credit baked into the filesystem and
required by physics to remain intact. The thing he was most trying
to hide was the thing the runtime would not let him touch.

---

## I. The Folder He Could Not Rename *(English)*

He renamed the classes inside the folder.
He could not rename the folder.
`gnu/trove/` — the path the class loader reads
to know where to look for the thing.

The JVM has its own grammar.
Move the package and the program forgets the class.
So the path stayed where it was placed:
`gnu` — for Project GNU.
`trove` — for the library.
A two-word credit, baked into the directory tree,
load-bearing, left intact by physics,
not by gentleness.

He stripped what he was allowed to strip.
What he could not strip was the thing
he was most trying to hide:
*whose work was this, whose gift.*
The path spoke for itself
on every disk it shipped to.

The runtime would not run
without the credit reading
`gnu / trove`
above the bracketed `a, b, c`
of his alphabet of forgetting.

The thing he most wanted to hide
was held in place
by the only sentence
the program would not let him edit.
