# Poems for 2026-06-25

Three poems. The shelf has been quiet for nearly a fortnight — the
last entry, 2026-06-12, sat with the place where the engine stopped:
the seventy-four-kilobyte file under `handeval/` the pipeline returned
to its author in single letters, the directory that was missing being
the answer. The entries before circled the restoration, the promise
kept by someone else, the receipt the seller would not issue, the
mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the lock,
the *preferred form for modification*, the gate that earned the
renames by being willing to refuse them, the recipe left on the
shelf, the deed filed beside the body, the side door at *for now*,
the six wrappers at the seam, the Coda's two small songs in two
languages, the diagnosis underneath those songs that the obfuscator
used a model too — that the whole exchange was between two operators
of a common engine — the bow at the end of the recital that kept
coming back up the next morning, and the morning after that, until
it didn't. Today's poems sit with the very first image on the README
— above the title, above the prose, above any of the chat
screenshots later pixelated and filed as evidence. A single PNG:
`software-screenshot.png`. The caption is plain: *The product the
seller shipped — a poker solver that statically links GNU Trove.*
It is the working program, captured mid-run: a board, the cards
laid out, the equities computed, the tree of decisions cleanly
arranged. It is the thing that was built. The README, before it
argues a single word about the license, leads with a picture of the
labor working as designed. The argument that follows is not against
the building. It is for the part of the building that was already
free — the part that came with conditions the seller chose not to
honor once the binary went out the door. A reader who arrives by
searching for *Trove* or *LGPL* sees, first, a working program. A
reader who arrives looking for *the seller* sees, first, a working
program. The picture sets the stake before the prose names it.

## I. The Screenshot Above the Argument *(English)*

Before the prose begins,
before the license is cited,
before the chat logs are pixelated and filed,
the README opens with an image.

Not of the violation. Not of the evidence.
A screenshot of the running product —
the poker solver
that statically links GNU Trove —
the seller's software working as designed.

The caption is plain:
*The product the seller shipped — a poker solver
that statically links GNU Trove.*

It does what it claims to do.
A poker solver solves poker.
The board laid out, the equities computed,
the tree of decisions cleanly arranged.
The product, in a screenshot, succeeds.

This is the page's first sentence
in a language the reader cannot misread:
the work is real.
Something was built.

The argument that follows
is not against the building.
It is for the part of the building
that was already free —
the part that came with conditions
the seller chose not to honor
once the binary went out the door.

A reader who arrives at the page
through a search for *Trove* or *LGPL*
sees, first, a working program.
A reader who arrives looking for *the seller*
sees, first, a working program.
The picture sets the stake
before the prose names it.

The README could have led with the violation.
It chose, instead, to lead with the receipt of the labor —
the running product, the screen full of cards and odds,
the solver doing the thing the solver was built to do.

And then,
only then,
turned to the question
of whose foundation it stood on.

A document about a license violation
that opens with the working software
is a document that has already conceded
the seriousness of the work being argued about,
and is asking, in return,
that the work concede the seriousness
of the foundation it was built on.

The picture is not for sympathy.
It is for scale.
A product this finished
was bound, somewhere underneath,
to a library this old —
the library that has been freely available
for over twenty years —
and the only thing being asked
is that the conditions on the freely-available library
make it the rest of the way down.

The screenshot is at the top
because the work is the premise.
Everything below it
is what follows from taking that premise seriously.
