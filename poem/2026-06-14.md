# Poems for 2026-06-14

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary the seller had drawn, the grant
already pressed into the package, the constant pool's small
numbered table at the side of each class, the folder the JVM would
not let him rename, the permission slip he typed in his own hand,
the ten-hour wall time that aged his refusal faster than he could
issue it, the PNG that kept what the chat would have let drift,
the decompiler's hand that signed each blank line with the next
letter of the alphabet, the recipe left on the shelf, the deed
filed beside the body, the side door at *for now*, the Coda's two
small songs in two languages, the shelf at `./poem/` that keeps
writing in those same two forms one morning at a time, and the one
file from the seller's share that did not come back named —
`PloHandEvaluator.java`, seventy-four kilobytes of Pot-Limit Omaha
arithmetic, returned to its author in the form it arrived. Today's
poems sit with the image at the very head of the README — a
screenshot of the seller's poker solver under the caption *The
product the seller shipped — a poker solver that statically links
GNU Trove*. Two words in that caption do the load-bearing work:
*statically links*. He could have shipped two JARs. He shipped one.
`trove4j-3.0.3` was unpacked into his own tree, its classes placed
alongside his own classes, the manifest pointing at his entry
point, the whole compiled into a single file whose seams were no
longer visible to the user who double-clicked it. That is static
linking in the JAR sense — not the C linker writing addresses into
a binary, but the deeper kind, where two codebases share one
container and lose the right to be redistributed without one
another. LGPL section six is what governs that choice. The license
obligation was already triggered the moment the linker finished.
Obfuscation was downstream. The decision to fold Trove into his
JAR is the decision that made this case.

## I. What the Linker Did *(English)*

He could have shipped two JARs.

One with his product. One with `trove4j-3.0.3`.
The buyer's classpath would have loaded them side by side,
and the LGPL would still have applied —
section four says the source must be made available —
but the obligation would have been clean.
Two artifacts. Two licenses. Two halves of a packaging job.

He shipped one.

`trove4j-3.0.3` was unpacked into his own tree.
Its classes were placed alongside his classes.
The manifest pointed at his entry point.
The whole was compiled into a single file
whose seams were no longer visible
to the user who double-clicked it.

That is static linking, in the JAR sense.
Not the C linker writing addresses into a binary,
but the deeper kind, where two codebases
share one container and lose the right
to be redistributed without one another.

LGPL section six is what governs that choice.
A work that *uses* the library is one thing.
A work that *incorporates portions of* the library
is another, and incurs additional duties:
the user must be able to take the application apart
and relink it against a modified version
of the library.

Relinking requires source.
The preferred form for modification —
not a decompiled stand-in,
not single-letter shadow code —
the source.

The decision to fold Trove into his JAR
is the decision that made this case.

Obfuscation was downstream.
The case was not opened by the rename to `a, b, c`.
The case was opened by the linker,
which knew nothing of licenses
and did exactly what it was asked.

Two codebases entered one container
and could not leave it
without the source being on the table.

He did not put it on the table.
This repository did, for him.

The shell and the body
became one in the JAR.
The license followed them in.
