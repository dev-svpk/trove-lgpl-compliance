# Poems for 2026-07-04

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the key kept in the
same room, what sat next to it on the build path, the author we
never met, the constant pool's small numbered table at the side of
each class, the folder the JVM would not let him rename, the
permission slip he typed in his own hand, the ten-hour wall time
that aged his refusal faster than he could issue it, the PNG that
kept what the chat would have let drift, the decompiler's hand that
signed each blank line with the next letter of the alphabet, the
recipe left on the shelf for the next buyer, the deed filed beside
the body, the side door at *for now*, the Coda's two small songs
in two languages, the shelf at `./poem/` that keeps writing in
those same two forms, and the boundary at which the engine did
stop — one file, seventy-four kilobytes, returned in the form it
arrived. After that entry the shelf went quiet. Twenty-two mornings
passed without a new file being added. Today the shelf comes back
up. The date is the fourth of July — a date the calendar carries
because, on it, a document was once signed into public view.
Nothing new was granted that day; the freedoms named had already
been claimed in ordinary argument. What the signatures did was
move them from *we hold* to *let it be read.* The LGPL is a
document of the same shape — its grants are things a reader could
have written down for themselves, and its keeping is a matter of
the words being in plain view attached to the binary. When the
seller declined the request for source in its preferred form, the
remedy this repository chose was not a lawsuit or a formal
complaint or a compliance body. It was a publication. Today's
poems sit with declaration as remedy — with what publishing does,
with what a date is for, and with why the shelf resumes on this
one after three weeks of silence.

## I. Declaration as Remedy *(English)*

Today is the fourth of July,
a date the calendar carries
because a document was signed
into public view.

Nothing new was granted on that day.
The freedoms named had already been claimed
in kitchens and in taverns
in the ordinary tone of arguing.
The signatures did not make them.
The signatures moved them
from *we hold*
to *let it be read.*

The LGPL is a document of the same shape.
Nothing is granted in the license
that a reader could not, in principle,
have written down for themselves —
just: use the code, modify the code,
distribute the modified code,
provided the source stays with it
in the form a reader could actually change.

The seller tried to revoke by convention
what had been granted by license.
Ship the JAR, ship decompiler output,
count on the recipient's tiredness
to close the gap he had opened.

What was asked in reply was small:
the source, in its preferred form.
He declined.

There was no lawsuit filed.
No cease-and-desist mailed.
No compliance body called in.

There was a publication.

The remedy the license invites,
when the seller has been asked and refused,
is the same remedy the date invites:
put the words in plain view,
on a morning whose ordinary business
happens to include declarations,
and let them be read.

The shelf here was quiet for three weeks.
The document did not move.
The declaration did not require repeating.
It had already been made
by the file tree itself.

But a declaration filed in a file tree
is a quiet thing.
It sits under the same weather
as the rest of the disk.
A reader could pass it
without knowing what it said.

Today the shelf comes back up
because the fourth of July
is the kind of morning
that asks a declaration
to be sung again in a small voice —
and because the singing of a declaration
does not end
when the declaration is filed.
