# Poems for 2026-07-01

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary the seller had drawn, the grant
already pressed into the package, the constant pool's small
numbered table at the side of each class, the folder the JVM would
not let him rename, the permission slip he typed in his own hand,
the ten-hour wall time that aged his refusal faster than he could
issue it, the PNG that kept what the chat would have let drift,
the decompiler's hand that signed each blank line with the next
letter of the alphabet, the recipe left on the shelf, the deed
filed beside the body, the side door at *for now*, the Coda's
two small songs in two languages, the shelf at `./poem/` writing
in those two same forms one morning at a time, and the one file
left in single letters at the door of the hand evaluator where
the engine found its limit. Nineteen mornings have passed since
the shelf last opened. On the first of a new month, it opens
again — not because anything moved. The seller is still silent.
The pixels still hold. No prospective customer, no other buyer,
no Trove maintainer, no FSF compliance contact has come to knock.
Today's poems sit with the mechanism the seller chose to deliver
what he sold: a Google Drive share. A link that opened onto a
folder no third party had cause to know about or means to reach.
Inside: the built JAR. Beside it, a directory the seller labelled
*full source code* — nine hundred `.java` files under `gnu/trove/`,
single-letter identifiers, decompiler shape without decompiler
notice. A Google Drive share is a private envelope, addressed to
one recipient, revocable from one side, leaving no public trace.
That shape suits a purely private transaction. It does not suit
a cargo whose license — LGPL-2.1 — was written for the opposite:
distribution that keeps distributing, source that travels with the
binary, terms that reach whoever the binary reaches. The envelope
did not survey what was inside it. This repository is the letter
unfolded — a `LICENSE` at the head of the tree, a `README` in
plain markdown, a file listing anyone can read, a history anyone
can pull, an issue anyone can open, nothing revocable from one
side. The share was for one recipient. The shelf is for anyone
who cares to walk up and read the spine.

## I. The Envelope and the Deed *(English)*

The delivery came as a Google Drive share.
A link, revocable from one side —
opening onto a folder no third party
had cause to know about
or means to reach.

Inside: the built JAR.
Beside it, a directory the seller labelled *full source code* —
`.java` files with single-letter identifiers,
the shape of source without its substance,
nine hundred of them under `gnu/trove/`.

`gnu/trove/` is not the seller's directory.
It is a directory whose name is a covenant.

LGPL-2.1: to distribute the library
is to distribute its *preferred form for modification*
alongside — on the same terms —
to anyone the binary reaches.

A private drive share is the opposite of that.

An envelope addressed to one recipient.
It does not travel further.
It closes from the inside at any hour.
It leaves no public trace,
no commit, no history of what changed,
no version pinned to a spot on a tree
where any later reader could stand.

That shape is fine
for a purely private thing.

It is not the shape
for what was given, decades ago,
to everyone at once,
under terms that require
the giving to keep on giving.

The envelope did not survey its cargo.
It was a mailing-mechanism
picked to fit a shape of commerce —
one buyer, one delivery, private terms —
and what was inside came under a covenant
the envelope was not built to carry.

This repository is the letter unfolded.

A `LICENSE` at the head of the tree —
the covenant supplied verbatim.
A `README` in plain markdown.
A file listing anyone can read.
A history anyone can pull.
An issue anyone can open.

Nothing revocable from one side.

The share was for one recipient.
The shelf is for anyone
who cares to walk up
and read the spine.
