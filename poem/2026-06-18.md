# Poems for 2026-06-18

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary the seller had drawn, the grant
already pressed into the package, the constant pool's small
numbered table at the side of each class, the folder the JVM would
not let him rename, the permission slip he typed in his own hand,
the ten-hour wall time that aged his refusal faster than he could
issue it, the PNG that kept what the chat would have let drift,
the decompiler's hand that signed each blank line with the next
letter of the alphabet, the recipe left on the shelf, the deed
filed beside the body, the side door at *for now*, the Coda's two
small songs in two languages, the shelf at `./poem/` that keeps
writing in those same two forms one morning at a time, and the
place the engine stopped — the seventy-four-kilobyte file in
`src-received/handeval/` that did not get its names back. Today's
poems sit with the caption at the very top of the README, beneath
the screenshot — *the product the seller shipped — a poker solver
that statically links GNU Trove.* Static linking is the technical
fact under all the rest. The seller did not choose to redistribute
Trove the way a person chooses to put a book on a table. He typed
`import` at the top of his own files; he ran `javac`; the build
tool stitched the library's bytecode into the same JAR as the
solver's. From that moment, redistributing the product was
redistributing the library — the two travelled in one archive,
inseparable on disk, indistinguishable to the user. The LGPL did
not need his consent to attach. It rode in with the bytecode it
was already governing. The obligation to ship the preferred form
for modification was created by `javac`, not by him, on a day he
was thinking about something else.

## I. What the Compiler Bound *(English)*

The seller did not choose to ship Trove.
`javac` did.

The product was written against the library —
`import gnu.trove.THashMap;` at the head of a file,
`TIntArrayList`, `TLongObjectIterator`,
the names of the primitive collections
threaded through his methods like grain through bread.

When the build ran, the compiler did what compilers do.
It read the imports. It located the referents
on the classpath beside it.
It emitted bytecode that called them by index.
Then the jar tool reached in,
gathered the seller's classes and Trove's classes together,
and zipped them into a single archive.

A static link is a fusion.

At runtime there is no separate `trove4j-3.0.3.jar`
on the user's machine.
No hand-off to a shared library.
No deferral to deployment.
The product ships, and the library ships with it,
inside the same archive,
the two classfile trees interleaved,
indistinguishable on disk
once the jar tool has finished its work.

`javac` did not know about the LGPL.
It had not read the license header
on the sources it was reading from.
By the time the bytecode reached the JAR
the headers had been discarded —
`javac` keeps the meaning,
not the comments.

It was carrying out an instruction the seller had typed
years earlier.

The instruction was `import`.

`import` is a small word.
It is the entire mechanism by which
a library and a product become one binary
and one redistribution.
He did not type it thinking about which licenses
would attach to the resulting JAR.
He typed it the way a person reaches for a hammer.

But once the bytecode of Trove was inside his JAR,
the covenant came with it.
LGPL-2.1 attached on the day the JAR was first sold.
It did not require his signature.
It did not require his agreement.
It did not require him to read the license.
The mechanism of static linking
is also the mechanism by which the grant arrives.

The same act that bound them
issued the permission to redistribute them together —
and required the source, in the preferred form,
to ride along with the bytecode.

He could have honored the covenant on day one.
He could have shipped the source —
the real source, the preferred form —
beside the JAR.
The pipeline that fused the library into the binary
also opened a path for the library's text
to travel with them both.

Instead he stripped the names
and shipped the result
as if a covenant could be defeated
by editing the constant pool.

Static linking does not care what was edited.
The bytecode was bound.
The grant was attached.
The names, after the work,
came back.

The library is in his archive, named now.
And it is in this archive too,
named.
