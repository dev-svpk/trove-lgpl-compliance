# Poems for 2026-07-17

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the
receipt the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of
repair, the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that
opened the lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search
yet to happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room,
what sat next to it on the build path, the author we never met,
what the program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the
seller receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing
to refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary the seller had drawn, the grant
already pressed into the package, the constant pool's small
numbered table at the side of each class, the folder the JVM would
not let him rename, the permission slip he typed in his own hand,
the ten-hour wall time that aged his refusal faster than he could
issue it, the PNG that kept what the chat would have let drift,
the decompiler's hand that signed each blank line with the next
letter of the alphabet, the recipe left on the shelf, the deed
filed beside the body, the side door at *for now*, the Coda's two
small songs in two languages, the shelf at `./poem/` that keeps
writing in those same two forms one morning at a time, the file
that did not come back through the pipeline, the two silences that
ran in parallel across a month, the month between two entries that
would otherwise not have mentioned it, and the record that needs
no audience to be a record. Today's poems sit with a noun. The
README calls him *the seller*. Not the author. Not the maker. Not
the developer. Not the licensor, though he did write a license
note. *The seller*, throughout. The word was chosen once and kept.
Someone who does not own a thing may still sell a copy of it — if
the thing was made in a way that lets copies travel, and if what
has to travel with the copy actually travels with it. LGPL is the
rule for what has to travel. He did not send what had to travel.
He sent instead the shape of what someone else had made with the
names removed, and called it source. The word *seller* holds all
of this without accusing anything. It does not say he stole. It
does not say he lied. It says only: he sold. A thing made by
another person, under a license that required something to travel
alongside it. One word does the work. The shelf did not need a
second.

## I. The Word "Seller" *(English)*

The record calls him *the seller*.
Not the author.
Not the maker.
Not the developer.
Not the licensor, though he did write a license note.
*The seller.* Throughout. One word, chosen once, kept.

The word is patient with him.
It does not say he stole anything.
It does not say he lied about what he owned.
It says only what he did.
He sold.

Someone who does not own a thing
can still sell a copy of it —
if the thing was made
in a way that lets copies travel,
and if what has to travel with the copy
actually travels with it.

Trove was made in a way that lets copies travel.
LGPL is the rule for what has to travel with them.
Source rides with the binary —
not decompiler output,
not the shape of the source with the names taken off,
but the *preferred form for modification.*
Whatever the maker was working from
when the maker made it.

The seller had never been the maker.
He had only ever been the seller.
When asked for the source, he did not have any to send.
What he sent was what he had —
the shape of the thing
with the names removed —
and he called it source.

The word *seller* holds all of this
without pressing any further.
It does not need to say the other things.
The word contains the case.

Someone whose relationship to a thing
is that he sold it,
and only that,
was authorized to sell it under one condition
that this shelf keeps writing down.
The condition was not met.
The word is what the record
kept using.

The choice was made on day one, in the README,
and never rewritten.
Every entry on this shelf that mentions him
mentions him by the same name.
No sharper word appeared,
because no sharper word was needed.
The word already fit.
