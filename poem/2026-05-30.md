# Poems for 2026-05-30

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary, the grant already pressed into
the package, the constant pool's small numbered table at the side
of each class, and the folder the JVM would not let him rename.
Today's poems sit with a single sentence the seller typed in a
chat — the one quoted at the top of the README. *"The code is sold
as-is, and is free to be redistributed, resold or used however
they see fit."* He thought he was wrapping the transaction up in
friendly language. He thought "as-is" was a shield, and "free to
be redistributed" a wave-of-the-hand reassurance to a paying
customer. What he actually did, in his own hand, was sign the
permission slip for the very act of redistribution this repository
performs. The case against him is — at the level of plain grant —
partly his own to author. The buyer is, in part, following
directions.

---

## I. The Permission Slip *(English)*

He said it himself, in plain language,
on a Tuesday afternoon in a chat —
the screenshot is in `evidence/`.

> *"The code is sold as-is, and is free to be redistributed,
> resold or used however they see fit."*

He thought "as-is" was a shield.
He thought "free to be redistributed" was a generosity,
a wave-of-the-hand reassurance to a paying customer.

What he did was sign the permission slip.

The Trove portion was already fastened to his binary
by a license he had not written —
the LGPL, which already said
the recipient may redistribute.
He had no standing to add or subtract that clause.
But he affirmed it. He pressed his own thumb to it,
in writing.

So when the buyer redistributed the package —
deobfuscated, on a public repository,
under the same license, as the LGPL provides —
the buyer was, in part, following directions.
The seller's own sentence sits above the act,
typed in his own hand,
screenshotted into the record.

It is not often
a refusal and a permission
come from the same mouth.

He gave away the very ground he meant to stand on,
in a sentence shorter than a paragraph,
and never knew he had done it.
