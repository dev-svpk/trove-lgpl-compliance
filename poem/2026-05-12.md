# Poems for 2026-05-12

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach, the receipt the seller
would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair, the small
mosaic kept over the seller's face, and the polite ordinariness of
the prompt that undid the lock. Today's poems sit with the
category error at the heart of the seller's claim: what comes out
of a decompiler is not source. It is a transcript of bytecode with
the names scrubbed off. The LGPL had a phrase ready for this
confusion long before the seller was around to try it — *the
preferred form for modification* — a clause that named this bluff,
in advance, in 1991.

---

## I. The Preferred Form *(English)*

The license has a phrase for what it wants:
*the preferred form for modification.*
Not text that looks like source.
Not text from which source can be inferred.
The form a person picks up when they want to change it.

A page of `a, b, c, d`
is not that form.
No one writes a library that way.
The seller did not write his that way either —
the decompiler did, and the decompiler does not write,
it transcribes: a copy of a lecture
with the names of the speakers scrubbed off.

The phrase was put into the license
by people who had seen this bluff before.
They wrote it in 1991.
They wrote it ahead of the seller,
who would meet it in 2026,
unaware that the clause
had been waiting for him for thirty-five years.
