# Poems for 2026-07-06

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the

happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the

receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary the seller had drawn, the grant
already pressed into the package, the constant pool's small
numbered table at the side of each class, the folder the JVM would
not let him rename, the permission slip he typed in his own hand,
the ten-hour wall time that aged his refusal faster than he could
issue it, the PNG that kept what the chat would have let drift,
the decompiler's hand that signed each blank line with the next
letter of the alphabet, the recipe left on the shelf, the deed
filed beside the body, the side door at *for now*, the Coda's
two small songs in two languages, the shelf at `./poem/` that
keeps writing in those same two forms one morning at a time, the
bow that keeps coming back up as a coda that refused to be the
last word, the reading of the Coda from the shelf that named two
operators of a common engine, and the place where the engine
stopped at the door of the hand evaluator. Today's poems sit
with the name of the middle folder itself — the directory the
file tree lists as `trove-de-obfuscate-mid-stage/`. The word
*mid* is a confession. It concedes, in its own name, that
another folder is expected next to it — and next to it there
is: `trove-de-obfuscate-final-stage/`. Six files sit under
that second folder — the wrapper classes: `IntList`, `LongList`,

mid-stage folder did not oversell what it had done. The
final-stage folder did not oversell what remained. Two folders.
Two truths. The apparatus confessing its own shape in the file
tree, before the reader had opened a single file inside either.

## I. The Word *Mid* *(English)*

The middle folder is called `mid-stage`.
The name gave the game away.

Next to it, in the file tree,
there is another folder called `final-stage`,
holding six files —
`IntList`, `LongList`, `CharList`,
`IntSet`, `LongSet`, `CharSet` —
and a `justfile` that runs them.

The first sweep took nine hundred classes.
The bytecode diff closed on each.
The token stream held.
The renames rode through, one after another,
because Trove's upstream vocabulary
answered the challenge every time.

Then six files did not answer.

They lived in `gnu/wrapper/`.
Their real names were also
`IntList`, `LongList` — the friendly interface
between the primitive-typed engine
and the code that used it.
They were called, by name, from the seller's own `solver/`,
which the first sweep was also renaming
in the lane next door.

The first sweep could not rename the wrappers
without watching the sweep rewrite
the very words the seller had typed
as call sites inside his product classes.
The two waves would have crossed each other
and the equivalence gates would have caught them at it
and thrown them both back.

So a second lane was cut.
A folder next to the first,
named for what it did: `final-stage`.
Six files. One `justfile`.
The same equivalence checks,
this time with the seller's `solver/` names held still
and the wrappers renamed
inside the frame of names that referenced them.

The two-lane pipeline is not glamorous.
It is the honest shape of an apparatus:
the first sweep, and the small careful sweep after,
for the pieces the first could not resolve
without breaking what it was supposed to preserve.

The naming of the middle folder as `mid`
was the honest confession
that the work had a next step.
`final-stage` is not a boast.
It is the second lane
picking up what the first lane
had to hand off.

Six files. Six wrappers.
The joint of the interface
between what the LGPL required to be readable
and what the seller had built on top of it.

The folder said: *this pass is not the last one.*
The next folder said: *here is the last one.*

Both names told the truth.
The file tree that opens the README
is the first place the reader learns
that the work knew where its own limits were,
and named them.
