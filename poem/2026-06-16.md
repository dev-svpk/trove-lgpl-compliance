## I. The Binding That Pulls You In *(English)*

Above the README's first sentence there is a screenshot.
Beneath the screenshot, a caption.
The caption is one sentence:
*The product the seller shipped —*
*a poker solver that statically links GNU Trove.*

Two of those words are doing the legal work.

*Statically links.*

A dynamically linked product loads its libraries at runtime —
the JAR sits next to the executable in its own file,
the executable calls into it across a runtime boundary,
the LGPL touches both sides with a light hand:
keep the library intact, ship it next to the product,
and the closed side stays closed.

A statically linked product folds the library in.
Trove's classes are not next door; they are in the binary.
The shipped artifact is a single object —
one JAR, one class loader, one file the buyer double-clicks.

The LGPL knows what that is.
When the library lives *inside* the artifact,
the artifact carries the library's license out into the world.
The whole envelope has to be redistributable in a form
that lets a recipient relink it.
Source for the Trove part is part of the deal,
in the *preferred form for modification,*
not whatever falls out of a decompiler.

The seller chose the binding that pulled him in.

It would have been less work, *legally,* to ship Trove as a
separate JAR
and call into it at runtime —
the LGPL is built to make that easy.
Static linking is the technical choice
that turns the legal shape of the product tight.

This repository exists because of the binding.
If the choice had been dynamic,
the obfuscation might have stayed inside the closed half —
the LGPL portion satisfied by handing over the upstream JAR
without ever needing to touch the product files.

But the obfuscation reached into the bound half too.
The single-letter names ran clean through `gnu/trove/`
because there was no seam between his code and Trove's;
they were one binary, one obfuscation pass, one envelope.

That envelope is what made the whole repository necessary.

Two words under a screenshot.
The legal predicate for a thousand `.java` files.
The reason the deobfuscation had to happen at all.

The seller did not write the LGPL into his binary.
He chose the binding that pulled the LGPL in.
