# Poems for 2026-07-07

Three poems. Earlier days circled the restoration, the promise kept
by someone else, the readers before and after, the document that
endured, the equivalence at the depth, the two folders set side by
side, what the obfuscator could not reach in structure, the receipt
the seller would not issue, the collapsing arithmetic of repair,
the mosaic kept over his face, the polite prompt that opened the
lock, the *preferred form for modification*, the search yet to
happen, the other buyers, the key kept in the same room, what sat
next to it on the build path, the author we never met, what the
program had to say aloud, the foreground emptying as the seller
receded, the gate that earned the renames by being willing to
refuse them, the solver that priced the unknown but misread a
fixed rule, the smooth surface of the running product, the engine
that did not stop at the boundary the seller had drawn, the grant
already pressed into the package, the constant pool's small
numbered table at the side of each class, the folder the JVM would
not let him rename, the permission slip he typed in his own hand,
the ten-hour wall time that aged his refusal faster than he could
issue it, the PNG that kept what the chat would have let drift,
the decompiler's hand that signed each blank line with the next
letter of the alphabet, the recipe left on the shelf, the deed
filed beside the body, the side door at *for now*, the Coda's
two small songs in two languages, the shelf at `./poem/` that
keeps writing in those same two forms one morning at a time, the
six small handles at the seam named back to themselves, and the
one file returned to its author still in single letters. Today's
poems sit with a coincidence of the calendar. July 7 is Tanabata
in Japan, Qixi in China — the one night of the year when two
stars kept apart by the Milky Way are permitted to cross and meet.
The whole tradition of the day is a covenant kept across a
distance that never closes. Two parties, separated by a river,
holding to a promise on their own, once a year, at a place they
have never actually shared. The LGPL is a covenant, too. It
travels between people who do not meet. Eric D. Friedman released
Trove more than twenty years ago under a text that reads: whoever
redistributes this, in any form, must ship the source with it —
the preferred form for modification, not whatever falls out of a
decompiler. A stranger took the library, wrapped it in a closed
product, stripped the names, and sold it. Another stranger — who
has met neither the author nor the seller — put the names back.
None of the three have ever been in a room together. The covenant
walked the twenty years on its own legs, carried by strangers,
kept by strangers, and the copy of the license text sitting at
the top of this tree is the bridge across the river that the
covenant was already going to walk.

## I. Three Stars, One Covenant *(English)*

Today is July 7.
In Chinese and Japanese poetry
this is the one night of the year
when two stars, kept apart by the Milky Way,
are permitted to cross the river and meet.
The whole tradition of the day
is a covenant kept across a distance
that will not close.

The LGPL is a covenant, too.

Eric D. Friedman released Trove
more than twenty years ago
under a text that says:
whoever redistributes this, in any form,
must ship the source with it —
the preferred form for modification,
not whatever falls out of a decompiler.

A stranger took the library,
wrapped it in a closed product,
stripped the names,
and sold it.

Another stranger —
who has met neither the author
nor the seller —
put the names back.

None of the three
have ever been in a room together.

The covenant walked
the twenty years
on its own legs,
carried by strangers,
kept by strangers.

At the top of this tree
sits a copy of the license text.
It is what the seller's package
was missing.
It is the bridge across the river
that the covenant
was already going to walk.

Tanabata is the night
when a promise kept over distance
gets to look itself in the face
for a moment
before the river returns.

This covenant does not get that moment.
Eric D. Friedman
will most likely never read this shelf.
The seller has gone silent
under a mosaic.
The reconstructor
is a stranger to both,
writing daily
in the language of a distance
that will not close.

But the covenant walked.
The names came back.
Three stars,
never in the same sky,
holding the same promise —
which is what a covenant is for.

Once a year
the sky lets them
appear to meet.
The rest of the year
they hold to the covenant anyway,
each on their own bank,
which is the harder,
and more common,
and more faithful
version of the crossing.
